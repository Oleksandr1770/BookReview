Chapter 1. You don’t see any elephants around here, do you?

  • Generally, the usability testing is watching people use the thing.
  • There are two types of tests: quantitative and qualitative.
  • “In quantitative you are interested in providing something and you do this by measuring things like success rate and time-on-task” (Krug, 2010).
  • Most usability tests are qualitative. These tests are unscientific and require fewer users.
  • Most of the usability problems are easy to find.
  • Usability testing can improve your designing skills.

Chapter 3. A morning a month, that’s all we ask

  • Do testing with three users once a month. The problems should be fixed during the next month.
  • “A morning a month” advice perfectly works with Agile, but it turns to “A morning a sprint”. The only issue is that testing is usually done by the developers.

Chapter 4. What do you test, and when do you test it?

  • Start testing as early as possible.
  • Test other similar websites to avoid problems while developing yours.
  • Test sketches and wireframes by asking users if they know what that page is supposed to be and how to do a particular task.

Chapter 5. Recruit loosely and grade on a curve

  • Watch a target audience but choose people with different skills.
  • A group of three users is enough to identify the most significant problems of the things that are tested.
  • If necessary people are hard to find, hire a recruiter who will help you to find a “focus group”.
  • Do not ask people who are involved in the development process to test your website.
  • If you want a “focus group” consisting of your website users, create an invitation on your Home page.
  • When you have to choose people from a large number of applicants, make sure that they have required skills and then explain what is expected from them.
  • You should have a substitute in case people do not come on the test day. You can have someone who is always nearby or who can test your website remotely.

Chapter 6. Find some things for them to do

  • Find the tasks that must be tested and expand them into scenarios.
  • You have about 35 minutes for the participant to spend doing tasks, so choose tasks to fit this time. Also have extra tasks in case the user finishes earlier.
  • Write a scenario that the user can follow.
  • Ask participants not to use search.
  • Print the scenarios for participants and observers.

Chapter 7. Some boring checklists

  • Three weeks before testing: choose the tasks to be tested, a “focus group” and a test room.
  • Two weeks before: get feedback on the tasks form developers and stakeholders, invite participants and team members.
  • One week before: Provide details on the test day, choose a standby participant, prepare software.
  • One or two days before: inform participants and observers, prepare scenarios, check the recording equipment and be ready to welcome participants.
  • Test day (before the first test): test equipment, create bookmarks for pages that are tested.
  • At the end of each test: save results.

Chapter 8. Mind reading made easy

  • A facilitator has two main roles: a tour guide who helps participants to move in a right way and a therapist who tries to make the participant think aloud.

Chapter 9. Make it a spectator sport

  • Try to involve as many people in your organization as possible.
  • Observers should not be in the same room as the participant because this may make participant stressed.
  • Observer’s job is to take notes and enjoy the process.

Chapter 10. Debriefing 101

  • All websites have some usability problems but try to focus on the most significant ones.
  • Ask developers and stakeholders to review the list of problems and choose the most important.
  • You can ask people to vote.
  • Discuss with the team how the problems can be fixed.
  • Summarize the result of testing into a short paragraph.

Chapter 11. The least you can do

  • If you can not completely solve the problem, fix at least its smallest part.
  • Don’t leave usability problems unfixed for the upcoming redesign.
  • Sometimes the best way to fix problems is to remove its cause from the page.

Chapter 12. The usual suspects

  • Home page should do its job. It should help the user find the right way to move to the necessary page.
  • Make noticeable things that are important for the user.
